Title:
A disease-agnostic computational pipeline for tolerogenic epitope design: case studies in immune thrombocytopenia and multiple sclerosis

Abstract:
Antigen-specific tolerogenic therapy offers the prospect of selectively restoring immune self-tolerance in autoimmune disease without broad immunosuppression, but rational computational design of tolerogenic epitope candidates remains underdeveloped. We present an open-source pipeline that scores peptide candidates from autoimmune disease target antigens against seven literature-grounded tolerogenic criteria — MHC-II binding zone, HLA allele promiscuity, disease-validated peptide proximity, IL-10 induction potential, IFN-gamma penalty, solubility, and human proteome cross-conservation — and assembles top candidates into codon-optimized multi-epitope mRNA vaccine constructs. Applied to immune thrombocytopenia (ITP) and multiple sclerosis (MS), the pipeline independently recovers known immunodominant protein regions without gold standard labels: in MS, overlapping 15-mers from the MBP83-99 and MBP131-145 regions (both targeted by the ATX-MS-1467 clinical product) dominate top rankings; in ITP, the C-terminal cytoplasmic tail of ITGB3 adjacent to the validated Peptide_82 region ranks highest for HLA promiscuity and IL-10 potential. Novel candidates outside existing gold standards — including ENPVVHFFKNIVTPR from MBP217-231 and a KEFAKFEEERARAKW cluster from ITGB3 aa749-765 — represent testable tolerogenic hypotheses. The pipeline is fully reproducible from public data sources, adds a new disease in two configuration files with zero code changes, and is available at [GitHub URL]. All outputs are computational hypotheses requiring experimental validation.

1. Introduction
Autoimmune diseases collectively affect approximately 5-8% of the global population and are characterised by the breakdown of immune tolerance to self-antigens. Current treatments, including corticosteroids, rituximab, and splenectomy for ITP, or interferon-beta and natalizumab for MS, suppress immunity broadly rather than targeting the pathogenic response specifically. Antigen-specific tolerogenic therapy — administering self-antigen peptides in a context that promotes regulatory T cell (Treg) induction rather than effector activation — offers the prospect of disease-specific immune re-education without compromising systemic immunity.
Proof-of-concept for this approach exists in multiple disease models. In ITP, Hall et al. demonstrated that a combination of GPIIIa peptides aa6-20 and aa711-725 suppressed anti-GPIIb/IIIa antibody responses and induced FoxP3+ Tregs in HLA-DR15 transgenic mice. In MS, the ATX-MS-1467 product — a mixture of four MBP peptides including the immunodominant MBP83-99 region — completed a Phase I trial showing reduction in MBP-specific T cell responses and was well-tolerated in secondary progressive MS patients.
Despite this clinical progress, no integrated computational pipeline exists for the systematic design of tolerogenic epitope candidates from autoimmune disease target antigens. Existing tools address components of this problem — NetMHCIIpan for MHC-II binding prediction, IL-10pred for cytokine induction scoring, BepiPred for B-cell epitope flagging — but are not assembled into a disease-agnostic workflow that incorporates tolerogenic scoring criteria grounded in the relevant experimental literature.
We present the Tolerogenic Epitope Design Toolkit (TEDT), an open-source Python pipeline that integrates MHC binding prediction, seven tolerogenic scoring criteria, and multi-epitope mRNA construct assembly. We apply it to ITP and MS as case studies, characterise the relationship between computational predictions and experimentally validated tolerogenic peptides, and identify novel candidate regions for experimental follow-up.

2. Methods
2.1 Antigen sequences and epitope data
Protein sequences were retrieved from UniProt via the REST API. For ITP: ITGA2B (P08514), ITGB3 (P05106), GP1BA (P07359), GP1BB (P13224), GP9 (P14770), GP5 (P40197). For MS: MBP (P02686), PLP1 (P60201), MOG (Q16653). Known T-cell and B-cell epitopes were retrieved from the IEDB query API filtered by disease term ("autoimmune thrombocytopenic purpura" for ITP, "multiple sclerosis" for MS).
2.2 MHC-II binding prediction
Overlapping 15-mer peptides were scanned from the primary antigen sequence (ITGB3 for ITP, MBP for MS) using a sliding window. Binding predictions were obtained via the IEDB tools API using NetMHCIIpan 4.0 across a panel of 12 HLA class II alleles: HLA-DRB1*01:01, *03:01, *04:01, *04:05, *07:01, *09:01, *11:01, *13:01, *15:01, HLA-DQA1*01:01/DQB1*05:01, HLA-DQA1*01:02/DQB1*06:02, and HLA-DPA1*01:03/DPB1*04:01. Alleles were selected to represent global HLA-DR diversity and ITP-specific susceptibility associations.
2.3 Tolerogenic scoring criteria
Seven criteria were implemented, each normalised to [0,1] and weighted in a composite score. Criteria and weights were derived from published experimental literature and are documented with primary citations in the repository.
Criterion 1 — MHC binding zone (weight 0.20): Rewards percentile ranks of 2-10% (the "Goldilocks" tolerogenic zone), penalises extreme binders (<2%) consistent with supraphysiological TCR activation, and assigns a small non-zero score to weak binders (>20%) to accommodate ITP autoepitopes, which are low-affinity MHC binders by design due to thymic escape (Sukati et al. 2007).
Criterion 2 — HLA promiscuity (weight 0.20): Fraction of the 12-allele panel for which percentile rank ≤10%. Rewards broad population coverage consistent with Tregitope properties.
Criterion 3 — Disease-validated proximity (weight 0.30): Substring overlap with experimentally validated tolerogenic peptides from published ITP or MS studies. Exact matches to confirmed tolerogenic peptides score 1.0; partial overlaps score proportionally. This criterion encodes prior experimental knowledge and is explicitly distinguished from the independent predictive criteria.
Criterion 4 — IL-10 induction (weight 0.08): Local Random Forest model trained on Nagpal et al. 2017 supplementary data (S4: 1,242 peptides; validated on S5: 1,082 peptides; AUC 0.91, noting ~30% sequence overlap between sets). Weight reduced from initial design following Hall et al. 2019 finding that tolerogenic suppression in the ITP model was IL-10 independent, mediated by FoxP3+ Tregs.
Criterion 5 — IFN-gamma penalty (weight 0.10): Inverted IFNepitope2 score. High IFN-gamma prediction penalised as inconsistent with tolerogenic outcome.
Criterion 6 — Solubility (weight 0.05): GRAVY hydropathy score. Hydrophilic peptides favoured; insoluble peptides fail to reach tolerogenic dendritic cells in vivo.
Criterion 7 — Human proteome cross-conservation (weight 0.07): Fraction of 9-mer windows appearing in the human reviewed proteome (UniProt Swiss-Prot). High cross-conservation associated with natural Treg engagement.
2.4 Construct assembly
Top-ranked peptides were selected using a positional diversity filter (minimum 10-residue separation between selected peptide start positions) to ensure regional diversity. Peptides were joined with GPGPG flexible linkers or AAY rigid linkers. Codon optimisation used weighted random sampling from human Kazusa codon usage frequencies with an 18-codon sliding GC window constraint targeting 50-60% GC content. Final constructs include Kozak 5'UTR, beta-globin 3'UTR, and poly-A tail, with manufacturing notes for m1Ψ nucleoside modification and dsRNA-depleted LNP formulation.
2.5 Calibration
Peptide-specific calibration checks were implemented against disease gold standards. For ITP: Peptide_2 (TTRGVSSCQQCLAVS) calibrated on disease proximity and JMX (low MHC affinity expected — thymic escape mechanism); Peptide_82 (ALLIWKLLITIHDRK) calibrated on composite rank and MHC zone score (genuine HLA-DR binder per Sukati 2007 Table 6). For MS: MBP83-99 and MBP30-44 calibrated on proximity and JMX given the 17-mer length incompatibility with 15-mer scanning.

3. Results
3.1 Independent regional recovery — MS
When disease-validated proximity (Criterion 3) is excluded and peptides are ranked by the remaining six criteria alone, overlapping 15-mers from the MBP83-99 window occupy ranks 1, 3, 4, 7, and 8 of 113 MBP candidates. Overlapping 15-mers from the MBP131-145 window occupy ranks 5, 10, and 11. Both regions correspond to ATX-MS-1467 components that have shown clinical activity. The pipeline identified these regions without any gold standard information, driven primarily by strong MHC binding zone scores (NetMHCIIpan percentile ranks in the 2-10% tolerogenic zone) and high human proteome cross-conservation.
3.2 Independent regional recovery — ITP
For ITP, the picture is qualitatively different and consistent with the known biology of ITP autoepitopes. Six of seven gold standard peptides rank in the bottom 20% of independent scores — expected, because Sukati et al. 2007 demonstrated computationally (and we confirm with NetMHCIIpan) that ITP immunodominant peptides are low-affinity MHC binders. Their immunodominance arises from thymic escape rather than efficient MHC presentation. However, neighboring 15-mers from the same protein regions emerge independently: the C-terminal cytoplasmic tail adjacent to the Peptide_82 region (ITGB3 aa749-765) occupies ranks 1-3 and 8, with the highest HLA allele promiscuity in the dataset (binding 8-9 of 12 alleles) and strong IL-10 induction scores.
3.3 Novel candidate predictions
Two candidates outside any gold standard achieved high independent scores across both diseases.
In MS, ENPVVHFFKNIVTPR (MBP aa217-231) ranks second by independent score with the highest IL-10 induction probability in the MS dataset (0.88) and the highest HLA promiscuity (0.75, binding 9 of 12 alleles). This region lies between the MBP131-145 and MBP140-154 ATX-MS-1467 components and has not been characterised for tolerogenic potential in the published literature.
In ITP, the KEFAKFEEERARAKW cluster (ITGB3 aa749-765) achieves the highest HLA promiscuity in the ITP dataset (0.67-0.75) and strong IL-10 scores (0.52-0.57), with perfect solubility. It sits 14 residues downstream from the validated Peptide_82 region (aa737-751) in the transmembrane-cytoplasmic junction of ITGB3.
These candidates represent testable tolerogenic hypotheses. Experimental validation using patient PBMC proliferation assays and cytokine profiling, analogous to the methodology of Sukati et al. 2007, would determine whether these regions contain immunologically relevant epitopes in ITP or MS patients.
3.4 Construct outputs
Full scoring with all seven criteria produced top-ranked multi-epitope constructs for both diseases. The ITP primary construct (ITP-ITGB3-GPGPG-10ep) incorporates 10 positionally diverse ITGB3 epitopes across 195 amino acids, 768 nucleotides, at 54.2% GC content. The MS primary construct (MS-GPGPG-10ep) similarly incorporates 10 MBP epitopes. Both include manufacturing specifications for m1Ψ modification and dsRNA-depleted LNP formulation consistent with tolerogenic mRNA design principles.

4. Discussion
The pipeline demonstrates consistent regional recovery of validated tolerogenic targets across two mechanistically distinct autoimmune diseases without gold standard information, while generating novel testable hypotheses from independent scoring criteria. The finding that exact experimental peptides are not recovered — but their protein neighborhoods are — is consistent with the biology of antigen presentation: MHC-II prediction operates at the level of binding potential, while the precise immunodominant peptide within a region is shaped by processing efficiency, protease activity, endosomal chemistry, and TCR repertoire, none of which are modelled here.
The ITP result specifically illustrates a known limitation of MHC-based epitope prediction for autoimmune disease. As Sukati et al. established, ITP immunodominant epitopes are selected not by high MHC affinity but by thymic escape — poor presentation during central tolerance induction allows autoreactive T cells to persist. NetMHCIIpan correctly predicts low binding for these peptides. The pipeline accommodates this through the disease proximity criterion (encoding prior experimental knowledge) and the thymic escape note in scoring documentation, rather than treating the low binding prediction as a failure.
Several limitations constrain interpretation of these results. All outputs are computational hypotheses derived from preclinical and early clinical literature. No criterion has been validated in the context of this specific pipeline. The IL-10 model, while achieving AUC 0.91 on an independent validation set, was trained on general T-cell epitope data and shows partial set overlap between training and validation. JMX cross-conservation is approximated by 9-mer proteome lookup rather than the full JanusMatrix algorithm. B-cell epitope risk is assessed by the Parker hydrophilicity scale rather than structure-based prediction. Expert immunological review of candidate peptides is required before any experimental follow-up.
The disease profile system enables extension to additional autoimmune conditions — Type 1 Diabetes (insulin, GAD65, IA-2), Celiac Disease (gliadin peptides), or Rheumatoid Arthritis (citrullinated peptides) — through configuration files alone, without code modification.

5. Availability
The Tolerogenic Epitope Design Toolkit is available at [GitHub URL] under MIT license. All data is derived from public sources (UniProt, IEDB). Cold-cache end-to-end reproduction from empty data directories completes in approximately nine minutes. Disease profiles for ITP and MS are included. Adding a new disease requires two JSON configuration files.
